# FedNeMo: Communication-Efficient, Privacy-Preserving Federated Fine-Tuning of Nemotron via Adaptive Differential-Private LoRA

---

## Abstract

FedNeMo is a federated fine-tuning framework engineered atop NVIDIA NeMo 2.0 and orchestrated through NVIDIA FLARE, designed to enable privacy-preserving, communication-efficient collaborative training of the Nemotron-3 Nano hybrid Mamba-Transformer Mixture-of-Experts model across institutionally siloed clinical datasets. The framework synthesizes four algorithmic innovations: (1) unsupervised outlier detection via Multi-Task Autoencoders for data quality assurance, (2) Federated Preprocessing via Aggregated Statistics (FedPS) for global data harmonization without raw data exposure, (3) Randomized LoRA Subparameter Updates (FedRand) for structural privacy amplification, and (4) Adaptive Differential-Private Quantization with Shannon Entropy client importance weighting for communication compression with formal $(\epsilon,0)$-differential privacy guarantees. Evaluated in a simulated multi-hospital Indian healthcare federation, FedNeMo achieves communication payload reductions exceeding 75% relative to standard 32-bit FedAvg baselines while rendering Optimization-Based, Generation-Based, and Analytics-Based Gradient Inversion Attacks computationally infeasible.

## 1. Introduction: The Structural Dilemma of Enterprise LLM Deployment

The integration of Large Language Models (LLMs) into high-stakes, highly regulated ecosystems such as healthcare, finance, and the legal sector introduces a profound structural paradox. Foundational models require continuous exposure to vast, domain-specific datasets to achieve the semantic nuance and reasoning capabilities necessary for clinical diagnosis, automated medical billing, or financial sentiment analysis. However, this very data remains strictly compartmentalized behind rigorous regulatory frameworks, compliance mandates, and institutional competitive boundaries. Centralizing this intelligence into a single repository is neither legally permissible nor operationally viable.

Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as the prevailing theoretical paradigm to resolve this deadlock, allowing decentralized institutions to collaboratively optimize shared model weights without ever transmitting raw, localized data. Despite massive investments in this architecture, the practical application of Federated Learning to the parameter-efficient fine-tuning of Large Language Models remains critically constrained by three simultaneous, unsolved bottlenecks:

1. **Privacy Leakage via Parameter Exposure.** The transmission of parameter updates during techniques like Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) exposes institutional training data to sophisticated adversarial extraction methodologies. Recent work on MineGrad (OpenReview, 2025) has demonstrated that malicious servers can use poisoned pretrained models or analyze the structure of LoRA updates to analytically reconstruct private inputs with high fidelity. The FET (Fully Expose Text) attack further proves that even text data, once thought less vulnerable due to sensitivity to token errors, can be reconstructed from embedding layer gradients (ACL Anthology, 2025). These are not theoretical risks: Gradient Inversion Attacks (GIAs) and Membership Inference Attacks (MIAs) represent production-grade threats to any federated LLM deployment.

2. **Communication Overhead at Scale.** The sheer communication bandwidth required to transmit high-dimensional parameter updates across hundreds of federated communication rounds fundamentally throttles the scalability of the system. Even with LoRA reducing trainable parameters to a fraction of the full model, a Nemotron-3 Nano model with 30 billion total parameters and 3.6 billion active parameters generates LoRA adapter weight differentials that, when transmitted at full 32-bit floating-point precision across 100+ federated rounds with 10+ participating institutions, accumulate to gigabytes of upstream network traffic. For healthcare networks operating on constrained institutional bandwidth, this is operationally prohibitive.

3. **Statistical Heterogeneity and Non-IID Drift.** The inherent statistical heterogeneity of decentralized data, often manifesting as severe non-Independent and Identically Distributed (non-IID) data drift, causes naive federated averaging algorithms (FedAvg) to produce degraded, globally divergent models that fail to generalize across local distributions. In clinical settings, this heterogeneity is structural: a tertiary oncology center generates fundamentally different data distributions than a rural primary health center, and naive averaging of their respective LoRA updates produces a model that serves neither population adequately.

The **FedNeMo** framework is introduced to decisively resolve these intersecting limitations. Engineered specifically atop the NVIDIA NeMo 2.0 framework and orchestrated through NVIDIA FLARE, FedNeMo establishes a secure, communication-optimized, and statistically robust methodology for the federated fine-tuning of the Nemotron-3 Nano hybrid Mamba-Transformer model. The architecture achieves this by synthesizing advanced data curation algorithms, federated preprocessing harmonization, randomized subparameter transmission, and dynamically adaptive differential-private quantization. The resulting system empowers a consortium of isolated entities to collectively train state-of-the-art reasoning models while offering mathematically verifiable privacy guarantees and drastically reducing communication payloads.
